Jennifer Flower's Blog / What I write when not doing other things

Who’s On First?

Overnight, liquor seeps into the little cavities between what I’m going to do and actually doing it, between what I was thinking about and why.

The decisions I made yesterday are back on the table.

Caffeine hangs around me for days instead of hours, alcohol pickles my ideas and makes my head weigh about twice as much as any head should.

The next morning I have slightly less will to live than usual.

The older you get, and the more you let slip in your health, the harder it is to judge what’s Usual. Is it Usual now, since I have a shot of tequila most evenings, to spend the morning questioning the decisions I made yesterday?

There are a lot of people out there bravely shaking up Usual – Tim Ferriss, Dave Asprey, I could name quite a few. They’re blazing new health trails and giving lots of interesting advice.

The thing is, most of them have not been old yet.

Could someone direct me to some old people who have redefined Usual positively, using some of the new methods? By old, I mean old people by young people’s standards but not by ours. Like 50s. 60s. 70s.

Boomers.

And I don’t mean professional athletes. Anyone who has dedicated their life to the constant improvement of their physical performance is not a fair comparison to most of the rest of us who have been living lives of the mind and a few other things besides.

What I want is someone about my age who’s doing quite well, thank you, while fighting a load of genetic opposition, and handling the exigencies of daily life with a job, maybe kids, and a partner who’s not that interested in making many sacrifices of habit to feel a hell of a lot better.

The way these young bodyhackers talk, it’s simple to set a higher standard of Usual no matter how old you are. You just do a little High-Intensity Interval Training (HIIT) and yoga, you take a few supplements, you drink Bulletproof coffee, and you eat like a caveman, but not slavishly.

If I didn’t already have some complex problems to be solved, that would be easy.

For examples, I’ve got passengers and when you've got passengers it changes the equation.

Passengers, unwanted microbes, are notoriously difficult to identify via testing. If they don’t want to be found, they will make it very hard for you to find them.

They’ll hang out in the crevices of your gut and god-knows-where-else, rolled up into some kind of impenetrable slime while they’re under assault, and they won’t come out until they need to, when there’s a profusion of simple carbohydrates out there and then they will double, triple, in numbers.

They kinda remind me of the scenes in the first Matrix movie, where the rebels are in their hovercraft in the bowels of the system and then they hear a Sentinel, a bug-like drone with tentacles, coming in search to destroy them. The rebels shut down their engines and stay reaaalllly reaaalllly quiet until the Sentinel moves on.

The rebels are the good guys in the Matrix. In my body, the rebels are the bad guys.

I don’t know their names yet, but right now I feel like I have a host of these aliens inside me who are not welcome.

DO YOU HEAR ME, ALIENS?

Unfortunately, they’re right and I'm wrong: For most of my life they actually have been welcome in the sense that my body has never put up enough of a fight, automatically, to keep them out. I have to keep fighting, over and over again.

Some of us are just too receptive.

I was too receptive to sperm, too.

Despite several forms of otherwise highly effective contraceptives, I got pregnant more than once.

The doctors examining me were impressed.

So, dear reader, what are we talking about here?

That’s the question that an interpersonal psychoanalyst is taught to ask very early in their training:

“What’s going on around here?” In my case, why do I keep allowing things into my body that screw me up?

What’s different about the interpersonal psychoanalyst’s question (vis-à-vis the, say, Freudian’s questions) is that it’s a genuine question about the field. I mean field in the same way that theorists refer to Field Theory.

Interpersonalists are not just interested in your unconscious process – which is notoriously impossible to report on directly – we’re asking about your environment.

What, as far as you can see, is going on around you?

What does it look like to you, and what does it look like to me, and what do other people say about it?

The interpersonal psychoanalyst is ready to hear a lot of versions over a long period of time of how the same situation looks. That’s the way brains work. Your brain changes itself, that is, loosely speaking, your mind, with each recall, each telling.

That’s how psychoanalysis works. It gives you the chance to change your mind by telling the stories, by asking pain-in-the-ass questions, and by retelling. That actually changes your brain.

So, to get back to my point. How do I make sense of what feels like a lifelong vulnerability to hostile occupation? Not by assault, but by stealth? An occupation in which teeny tiny life forms take up residence in small ways, right alongside the ones that are supposed to be there, and gradually make their homes in me for the long haul?

I often don’t notice them until they’ve gained in numbers and have established habits.

Now, there are several ways of looking at this how-to-make-sense-of question. There are the physical properties and circumstances that predispose you to infection, like many years on and off antibiotics that purge you of the healthy flora in your gut, along with whatever infections that may have been plaguing you, to the point where you never really reestablish the internal conditions that routinely ward off invaders.

As we all know now, the problems with ingesting too many antibiotics (directly or indirectly) have been well studied and documented. That we've been taking way too many antibiotics is not a subject of much controversy any more. It’s one of the reasons we, as a society, have been cracking down on the unnecessary use of antibiotics in our food supply.

Another way to make sense of what's going on with you, a way that’s often controversial, is the relationship between, on the one hand, your brain, including what you were born with and how you’re changing it through exposure to chemicals and other physical properties, and on the other hand, your mind, your psychology.

Most people like to think of them as separate, even when they claim they don't think of them as separate. It’s a Western Mind habit to differentiate between your mind and your brain as though they were two different causes.

But that’s silly, isn’t it?

Think about Toxoplasmosis, the bug that infects many cats and rats and people, to name a few. It makes female rats more dependent, it makes male rats more impulsive, it changes how animals behave in order to make their own reproduction more likely (to learn more about how this works, see this story in the Atlantic Monthly).

So the feeling I often have, when I’ve got a lot of passengers on board – that my mood is different, my thinking is slightly different, and I crave carbohydrates – that’s not crazy, that’s a rational inference from a lot of new science. We now know that bugs change the way animals behave.

But what we also know is that our notions of reality, what we believe, can also change our physiology. In fact, there’s an entire specialty in science, called psychoneuroimmunology that bears on the interrelationships between mental events and the body. And there’s much in the scientific literature about beliefs and their impact on the body.

But that’s the subject of another post.

In the meantime, I recommend you read Moises Velasquez-Manoff’s book, An Epidemic of Absence. He talks about the internal bug situation at length. He starts to get at what it is about the society we live in now that might make it easier for microbes to colonize us without much pushback from our current immune systems that no longer function the way they used to in previous eras of human evolution.

I also recommend you listen to Ed Yong on the TED Hour episode called Hidden. It’s a fascinating talk about mind-controlling parasites. Yong’s also has a great book out called I Contain Multitudes. It’s about the partnership between animals and microbes.

Let me know what you think.
